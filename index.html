1.1 Jitter Plots

Explain in your own words the point of the jitter plot.

When you plot data as points with only one attribute, you can get in trouble with scatter plots, since data can overlap. Jitter plots are a way to overcome this issue by implementing a random generated shift (the jitter) perpendicular to the attribute(1). The shift of course has to be relatively small compared to the scale of the attribute, since the jitter can be mistaken for a 2D scatter plot otherwise.

1.3 Kernel Density Estimators

When can KDEs be misleading?

Kernel Density Estimators (KDE’s) are defined as(1)

The density, Dh, is a function of position, x, and data point positions, {xi}, with a smoothness defined by h. Now, the smoothness parameter can be estimated by minimizing error in a cross validation procedure, but it is not possible just from inspection to tell whether this has been the case for the choice of h. 1.4 The Cumulative Distribution Function

Janert writes ”[CDFs] have less intuitive appeal than histograms of KDEs”. What does he mean by that?

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) have the strength of showcasing in probabilities by interval. This comes at a price. Reading the probability of the interval is done by subtracting the intensities of the starting point and the end point in the chosen interval. Therefore, interpretation of the graph is not immediate and intuitive.

1.5 Quantile Plot

What is a Quantile plot? What is it good for?

Quantile plots are for comparing two datasets. By sorting the data points and plotting points with the same rank in the sort against each other, you get a quantile plot(1). Quantile plots can be used to compare whether two datasets are distributed equally and you can compare relative skewness of the two sets.

1.7 Summary Statistics

Explain why one has to be careful when using those - and why visualization of the full data is always better.

Summary statistics have the issue of being identical for a lot of different relationships. As showcased in DAOST, the mean is equivalent for both a linear trend with an outlier and a clean non linear trend(1). Thus, summary statistics fails often. The trends become immediately apparent from visualizing - even if the summary statistics works.

1.8 Box Plots

When are box plots most useful?

Box plots show a lot of information. The median (the middle of the sorted data) and the interquartile range from (from quantale 1 to 3) are plotted as a box, and to extend it to a whisker plot, the 5th and 95th quantile are lotted as error bars from the box(1). The box plot shows what interval the centered half of the data is positioned and the whiskers show the same just for 90% of the data. Box plots are very useful for data where most of the data is centered around mean/median but outliers would distort a distribution.

1.9 Violin Plots

The book doesn’t mention violin plots. Are those better or worse than box plots? Why?

Violin plots are box plots with a fitted normal distribution on each side instead of a box to symbolize the interquartile range(2). Violin plots convey more information as they include median and mean, variance and quantiles, and the skewness of the fitted normal distribution. However, the information conveyed is not made the most intuitive way, so splitting into a box plot and histogram with a fitted normal density will illustrate your point more immediately.

2.3 Smoothing Noisy Data

What are the two methods Janert metions for smoothing noisy data? Can you think of other ones?

Janert mentions two methods; splines and LOESS. Splines are about splitting up regionally, making local polynomials and joining them again in a smooth way by making an optimization goal globally(3). Thus, the local smoothness criteria is at the joints and the global smoothness criteria is optimized. LOESS is more locally focused. 2.4 Residuals

What are residuals? Why is it a good idea to plot the residuals of your fit?

Residuals are the absolute value of the difference between your fit and your actual target(3). Plotting residuals gives the observer a sense of where the error is high and low in your fit locally, which gives an overview of your performance of the solution.

2.5 Smooth Tubes

Explain in your own words the point of the smooth tube in figure 3-7.

Smooth tubes are basically a splitting of the smoothing methods into positive and negative residuals, which will introduce curves on each side on the original fit. Those curves can then act as confidence intervals(3), which also gives an overview of local performance of the solution.

2.8 Banking

What the h#ll is banking and what element from our visual system does it use to help us see patterns? What are potential problems with banking?

Banking is about changing aspect ratio so the slopes are in an approximate 45 degree angle(3), so the slope because a lot easier for us to process. In banking, aspect ratio of the plot can get too compressed for us to properly see the figure. If the figure gets too compressed, then cut-and-stack can be used to reduce a scale while still being able to use banking(3).

2.9 Figure 3-14

I think figure 3-14 makes an important point about linear fits that is rarely made. What is it?

Figure 3-14 in DAOST is about making linear regression with opposite features and targets (x on y and y on x)(3). The point is that the regression fits are not the same, because the data is not completely (100%) linearly correlated, and thus not the best fit after all.

2.10 Discussion of Graphical Analysis and Presentation Graphics

Summarize the discussion of Graphical Analysis and Presentation Graphics on pp. 68-69 in your own words.

The discussion are about best practices for analyzing data with graphs versus presenting results with graphs. Basically the recommendation are the opposite for both. Do not use time explanatory texts or effects for graphical analysis, but most certainly do it for presenting graphics. Making well explained graphs, Janart includes a list(3) of items which basically is;

  • basic information on graph,
  • include axis labels,
  • no nonstandard abbreviations,
  • pick suitable font and font-size,
  • explain error bars,
  • choose appropiate plot ranges,
  • proofread and
  • use scalable formats.

At last, taste can be acquired by studying graphs, which visualization is all about.

Boston Marathon winning times 1897-2017

Figure 1: Boston Marathon winning times 1897-2017 plotted for male runners, and female runners where applicable. Dashed line segments show linear fits that were applied to the data leading up to 1990.

Figure 1 attempts to visualize the overall trend in winning times at the Boston Marathon from 1897 to 2017. Data points are colored in by runner's gender. A linear fit has been applied to a subset of the data-- 1897-1990 for male runners, 1966-1990 for female-- via the least squares method and is visualized as a dashed line segment for each gender.
